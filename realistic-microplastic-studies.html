Why Many Current Experimental Studies May Not Fully Reflect the Real Hazard of Microplastics

A short research note on the gap between laboratory exposure and real aquaculture conditions.

Home Research Notes Publications Profiles

Research note based on the systematic review: Impact of micro and nanoplastics on aquaculture animals.

Why this question is important

Many laboratory studies show that microplastics and nanoplastics can harm aquatic animals. These particles may cause oxidative stress, tissue damage, immune disturbance, reduced growth, and other biological effects. These findings are important because they show that plastic particles can be hazardous.

However, an important question remains: do laboratory experiments always reflect the real hazard in aquaculture farms? The answer is not always. Some experiments are useful for detecting possible effects, but they may not represent the conditions that animals face in ponds, tanks, cages, or coastal farms.

The concentration problem

One major issue is exposure concentration. Many experiments use microplastic levels that are much higher than those measured in real aquaculture environments. High doses can help scientists detect biological changes in a short time, but they may overestimate the real-world risk.

This does not mean that the experiments are useless. They help identify possible mechanisms of harm. But for risk assessment, exposure levels should be closer to the concentrations found in real aquaculture sites.

The particle type problem

Another issue is the type of plastic used in experiments. In the environment, microplastics are often fibers or fragments. Common polymers in aquaculture areas include polypropylene and polyethylene. These particles may come from ropes, nets, cages, packaging, feed bags, and other plastic materials.

In contrast, many laboratory studies use clean spherical plastic beads, especially polystyrene beads. These particles are easy to buy and use in controlled experiments, but they may not behave like real environmental microplastics. Shape, size, polymer type, surface roughness, and weathering can all influence toxicity.

Exposure route matters

Microplastics can reach aquaculture animals through water or feed. These two routes may cause different effects.

Waterborne exposure may affect gills, respiratory surfaces, external tissues, and oxidative stress pathways. Dietary exposure may affect the gut, feeding performance, tissue structure, growth, and nutrient use. If a study focuses on only one exposure route, it may miss part of the real risk.

Short studies may miss long-term effects

Many experiments are short. They may last a few days or a few weeks. But animals in aquaculture may face low levels of microplastics for much longer periods. Long-term exposure may slowly affect growth, immunity, reproduction, and general health.

For this reason, future studies should use longer exposure periods, realistic concentrations, relevant particle shapes, common environmental polymers, and farm-related exposure routes.

Main message

Laboratory studies are necessary, but they should become more realistic. They should answer not only whether microplastics can cause harm, but also when, how, and under what real aquaculture conditions they become a true risk.

Mohsen, M. et al. Impact of micro and nanoplastics on aquaculture animals: a systematic review. Aquaculture International, 33, 655.
DOI: 10.1007/s10499-025-02323-y
