How Microplastic Fibers Transfer from Water to the Internal Fluid of Sea Cucumbers

A short research note explaining one pathway by which microplastic fibers can enter sea cucumbers.

Home Research Notes Publications Profiles

Research note based on the article: Microplastic fibers transfer from the water to the internal fluid of the sea cucumber Apostichopus japonicus.

Why this topic matters

Microplastics are very small plastic particles found in many aquatic environments. One common type is the microplastic fiber. These fibers may come from synthetic clothes, ropes, nets, fishing materials, and other plastic products. Because they are thin and light, they can stay in water and may come into contact with aquatic animals.

Sea cucumbers are important animals in marine ecosystems. They feed on sediment, recycle nutrients, and help improve the condition of the sea bottom. They are also valuable aquaculture animals. For this reason, understanding how microplastics affect sea cucumbers is important for both environmental protection and aquaculture.

The unusual breathing pathway

Sea cucumbers breathe in a special way. Water enters through the anus and moves into an internal organ called the respiratory tree. This organ helps the animal exchange gases with the surrounding water.

This means that sea cucumbers may be exposed to particles in the water not only through feeding, but also through respiration. If microplastic fibers are present near the animal, they may enter with the water during breathing.

What the study found

The study showed that microplastic fibers can move from the surrounding water into the internal fluid of the sea cucumber, called the coelomic fluid. The fibers entered with water during respiration. Some fibers became trapped in the respiratory tree, while others passed into the coelomic fluid.

This is an important finding because it shows that microplastics may reach internal body fluids through a direct water-respiration pathway. This pathway is different from the common idea that microplastics only enter animals when they eat contaminated food or sediment.

How the sea cucumber responded

The sea cucumber showed a defense response. One immune-related enzyme, called lysozyme, increased after the fibers entered the internal fluid. This suggests that the animal recognized the fibers as foreign material.

However, the study did not find major short-term changes in movement, such as speed or total distance moved. This means that the fibers caused an internal response, but the short-term behavior of the animal did not clearly change under the tested conditions.

Why this is important

The study shows that waterborne microplastic fibers can enter sea cucumbers through respiration and reach the internal fluid. This means that water quality around sea cucumber farms and natural habitats is very important.

It also suggests that microplastic pollution should not be studied only in sediment or feed. Microplastics suspended in water may also be an important exposure source for sea cucumbers and other aquatic animals with similar exposure pathways.

Source article:
Mohsen, M. et al. Microplastic fibers transfer from the water to the internal fluid of the sea cucumber Apostichopus japonicus. Environmental Pollution, 257, 113606.
DOI: 10.1016/j.envpol.2019.113606
